The Night 10,000 Punks Were Given Away for Free

March 2026  ·  Punksierge

Matt Hall and John Watkinson weren't trying to change the world. They were just bored.

The two ran Larva Labs — a tiny, two-person indie software shop with no VC money, no roadmap, and no hype. Between small mobile games and experimental side projects, they kept returning to the same question: what if you could generate thousands of unique characters — each one genuinely different, each one belonging to exactly one person on earth — and put them on a blockchain so nobody could fake ownership, copy them, or take them away?

So they built it. A generator that spit out 10,000 tiny 24×24 pixel faces. Mohawks. Cigarettes. Beanies. Gold chains. Tiaras. Some faces loaded with personality. Some completely bare. All of them inspired by London punk culture, cyberpunk fiction, the gritty anonymity of city life. They named the project CryptoPunks, deployed it to the Ethereum blockchain on June 23rd, 2017, and did something that would look insane in hindsight.

They gave all 10,000 away for free.

2017. Think about that.

Ethereum was barely two years old. MetaMask — the browser wallet most people use today — had just launched in 2016 and was almost unknown outside hardcore crypto circles. To claim a Punk, you needed an Ethereum wallet, you needed to understand what gas was, and you needed to know how to interact directly with a smart contract. This wasn't clicking "buy now." This was deep in the weeds of early internet infrastructure, the kind of place only the truly curious ever found.

For the first few days, barely anyone showed up. Then a Mashable article hit. Within 24 hours, all 10,000 were gone.

This is what crypto is supposed to be about.

Not expensive art auctioned to hedge funds. Not celebrities flipping JPEGs for a quick cash grab. Larva Labs charged nothing. No whitelist, no presale, no VIP tier. You showed up, you claimed, it was yours.

That's the beauty of it — and punk holders will tell you this themselves. The fact that these were free is inseparable from what makes them meaningful.

CryptoPunks represent what the crypto movement was always supposed to stand for: access, ownership, and power redistributed to the people. No gatekeepers. No middlemen. No institution deciding who gets in. Just a wallet, a few cents of gas, and a piece of internet history — if you were paying attention.

The early holders weren't wealthy collectors. They were coders, tinkerers, and true believers who stumbled into something real before the rest of the world arrived. Being that early is a gift. But it comes with a catch.

When you're that early, you just don't know.

Somewhere in that first wave, a single wallet moved with unusual precision. While most people casually claimed one or two Punks out of curiosity, this one was hunting. By the time the dust settled, it had accumulated 7 of the 9 Alien Punks in existence — plus 8 Apes. Out of 10,000 Punks, this person held more ultra-rare pieces than anyone else on earth.

That wallet belongs to someone now known as straybits.eth.

He eventually sold. Of course he did — nobody had the context to know what not to sell yet. The Aliens went for $3,000 to $4,000. The Apes, a few thousand more. At the time, that probably felt like a win.

In February 2022, a single Alien Punk sold for $23.7 million. Straybits had held seven of them.

This isn't a story about a bad decision. It's the brutal, humbling truth of being early: you can be the most dialed-in person in the room and still walk away with a fraction of what you held. The hardest thing in crypto — maybe in any market — isn't finding the right thing. It's holding it when nothing around you confirms that you should.

But let's talk about the art for a second.

Because beyond the floor price and the sale records, there's something genuinely beautiful about what Larva Labs built. The generator was creating combinations at random — traits assigned through probability formulas, thousands of possibilities colliding in ways nobody could predict or control.

Sometimes the machine just gets it right.

Punk #3684 is one of the best examples of this. The community calls her the Princess Punk. She's a female Punk with four traits: a gold earring, a tiara, red lipstick, and a gold chain.

On paper, that's just a list. But when you see how those four elements land together on a 24×24 pixel face, something clicks. There's an elegance to it that feels almost impossible — like it shouldn't work that perfectly, and then it just does, completely.

The rarity formula that governs trait combinations means the odds of these exact four coming together this way were vanishingly small. She's currently listed for 2,000 ETH. And when you look at her, you understand why.

Nobody designed the Princess Punk. The machine made her. And somehow, she's perfect. That's the other side of CryptoPunks that gets buried in the price discourse — these are artworks. Accidental, algorithmic, irreproducible artworks that belong to exactly one person on a blockchain that will outlast any of us.

Free. And worth everything.

That's the CryptoPunks story. Ten thousand pixel faces, given away for nothing in 2017 by two bored programmers who just wanted to try something strange. Claimed by a handful of people curious enough to figure out MetaMask before anyone else cared. Now some of them are worth more than houses, more than paintings, more than almost anything in digital history.

The people who got in early weren't lucky. They were paying attention when the rest of us weren't. And the fact that it was free — that the barrier was curiosity and nothing else — is exactly why it matters.

Power to the people. That's what this was. That's what it still is.
