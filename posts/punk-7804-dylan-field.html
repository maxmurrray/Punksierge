The Guy Who Sold Punk #7804 for $7.5M — and What He's Doing Now

March 2026  ·  Punksierge

In March 2021, the NFT market was losing its mind.

Beeple had just sold a JPEG for $69 million at Christie's. Twitter was full of pixel avatars. And a 29-year-old tech CEO named Dylan Field — co-founder of the design tool Figma — quietly listed one of the rarest NFTs in existence for sale.

The punk was Punk #7804. An Alien. One of only nine in the entire collection of 10,000. Wearing a cap turned forward, small shades, and a pipe — a combination of traits that somehow lands as unmistakably cool. Field had been holding it for years. And when the market hit a fever pitch, he let it go.

The sale price: 4,200 ETH. Approximately $7.57 million. The biggest CryptoPunk sale ever recorded at that point in time.

Who is Dylan Field?

If you don't follow design or Silicon Valley, you might not know the name. Field dropped out of Brown University on a Thiel Fellowship — Peter Thiel's famous $100K grant for young people who skip college to start companies — and went on to co-found Figma in 2012. For years, Figma was a quiet, beloved tool in the design world. Then it wasn't quiet anymore.

When he sold Punk #7804 in March 2021, Figma was already growing fast. The punk sale made headlines, landed him in crypto circles he probably hadn't been in before, and cemented his reputation as someone who understood where culture was heading. But the story of the punk is almost a footnote compared to what came next.

In September 2022, Adobe announced it was acquiring Figma for $20 billion — one of the largest software acquisitions in history. Field stood to make billions personally. The deal was ultimately blocked by European regulators in late 2023, which meant Figma stayed independent. Figma then filed for an IPO. Either way, Dylan Field is now worth somewhere in the range of a few billion dollars.

The $7.57 million from Punk #7804 is a rounding error on his net worth. But the punk? That's a different story.

What happened to the punk after he sold?

Punk #7804 changed hands and eventually landed with a collector known as artudito.eth — who has held it and, as of writing, has not listed it for sale.

The market has spoken clearly about what it thinks it's worth. In early 2025, a bid of 7,230 ETH — over $16 million — was placed on the punk and then withdrawn. That's more than double what Dylan Field sold it for. In dollar terms, whoever holds #7804 is sitting on something that has only grown in cultural weight since 2021.

Alien Punks have become the benchmark. There are only nine of them. Each one has a distinct identity in the community. #7804 — with its cap, pipe, and shades — feels like a character that wandered in from a different dimension and decided to stay. When people think about what a CryptoPunk is supposed to feel like, this is one of the images that comes to mind.

Did he sell too early?

On paper, yes — the punk has been bid at more than double what he received. But this question misses the point of who Dylan Field is. He didn't need the money then, and he definitely doesn't need it now. He sold at the top of a market he saw coming before most, for a price that would have changed most people's lives permanently, and then went back to building one of the most important software companies of the last decade.

He wasn't selling because he needed to. He was selling because he understood timing — and that's a rare skill in any market.

What makes the Dylan Field / Punk #7804 story interesting isn't the regret angle. It's what it says about the kind of people who were in CryptoPunks early. This wasn't a collector who stumbled onto something by accident. This was a builder — someone who recognized cultural value before the mainstream arrived — using a blockchain asset to signal exactly that, then walking back to his actual work.

The punk went on without him. Both are doing just fine.
